Architecture & Design — Source of Truth

The Anonymous Recipient Surface: Disclosure Matrix & Design Principles

How Onetime Secret balances a humane recipient experience against the duty not to leak metadata to an attacker. The recipient screen at the end of a one-time link must be kind to a confused human and silent to a probing adversary at the same time.

Issue #3424 Zero-knowledge constraint Anonymous surface: GET /api/v3/guest/secret/:id Status: design source of truth

“Humanize the framing, not the facts.”

Tone, warmth, and reassurance are free — the recipient screen can be as kind as we like. Differentiation is not free — any per-reality fact is an oracle. So the anonymous surface answers exactly one bit, and only affirmatively: “here is a secret to reveal” (HTTP 200), or one uniform terminal screen for everything else.

The truth of which reality occurred lives only on the authenticated owner surface, where it is the owner’s own data behind auth. That single split is what lets us be uninformative to strangers without being dishonest as a product.

Refinement — Three Parties, Not Two

A second, opportunity-seeking round corrected a framing bias: the first analysis saw two parties — the product vs. a hostile prober — and optimized to tell the prober nothing. There are three, and it collapsed the middle one into the threat.

🔐 Sender / owner

Authenticated; entitled to full truth about their own secret. A separate surface, behind auth.

👤 Legitimate recipient

The privileged holder: their own browser already possesses the plaintext, the reveal instant, and proof of winning the race — facts the server can never re-emit and an attacker can never forge.

🎯 Unknown prober

Anonymous, hostile, probing IDs at scale. Must learn nothing. The original (correct) subject of the uniform-response design.

“The server must stay a blind oracle to strangers — but the recipient’s own browser is allowed to remember what it already saw.”

Disclosure is a function of WHO is asking and WHAT they already hold — not a property of the response. So the desirable inverse of each privacy harm is not “disclose less,” but “disclose to the holder, client-side, in a way the server cannot witness.” The success case (b) is the one reality we can safely disambiguate to the recipient — precisely because only their browser proves it.

The Convergence

Six mutually-exclusive realities sit behind a “not viewable” result. The same fact that is a gift to the owner is an oracle to the anonymous visitor — so the two audiences must see different things.

👤 Anonymous recipient

One byte-identical “not available” screen for all six. No status, copy, size, timing, header, or branding difference. The visitor cannot tell success from typo from defect.

The full truth: exact state and timestamps (opened / revealed / burned / expired). It is their own data, behind auth — no oracle risk.

Disclosure Matrix

The default is one uniform response for every non-viewable reality. There are zero exceptions on the anonymous path — including the tempting one, case (b), “good news, it worked.” That confirmation (the ID existed, was consumed, roughly when) is precisely what an attacker wants.

a Expired Yes — TTL elapsed Uniform screen “Expired — never opened” + time Differs from (d) only by existence → existence oracle
b Consumed ✓ Yes — on the owner path Uniform screen “Opened & revealed” + when Leaks existence + view-status + timing of someone else’s secret

d Never existed Cannot distinguish from a/b/c Uniform screen N/A — not their secret Must look identical to a real-but-gone ID, or it is an enumeration oracle

Anonymous = the unauthenticated visitor at the secret link. Owner = the authenticated sender on their receipt/dashboard, a separate surface. The owner column is the pressure-release valve: it lets the product be honest about every outcome without the anonymous screen ever having to be.
Recipient self-disambiguation (round 2): exactly one reality — (b) SUCCESS — may be disambiguated to the legitimate recipient, and only by their own browser’s local record, never by the server. The other five stay uniform to everyone. This is the single safe exception, and it lives entirely client-side.

Philosophical Insights

What the adversarial design panel surfaced underneath the mechanics — the ideas worth keeping even as the implementation changes.

I.  FALSE CERTAINTY IS THE WOUND

The screen asserts the most where it knows the least.

Today’s “This secret has been viewed or expired” is a confident claim on an anonymous 404 the server cannot actually disambiguate. Honest uncertainty is a container the mind can rest in; false certainty that contradicts the user’s experience reads as a lie — and corrodes trust in the copy that is true.

II.  SUCCESS IS CAMOUFLAGED AS FAILURE

The product’s one win condition wears a red error.

A secret read once and destroyed is the system working perfectly — yet it renders identically to a typo or a defect. The better the product works, the more “failure” screens it produces. Never put the success case in the error channel.

III.  DISCLOSURE IS A FUNCTION OF WHO

The same fact is a gift to one audience and an oracle to another.

“This was opened at 14:02” is reassurance for the owner and surveillance for a stranger. The question is never just what is true, but to whom we may say it. Identity, not data, is the axis of disclosure.

IV.  ZERO-KNOWLEDGE ≠ ZERO-OBSERVABILITY

We can’t read the secret — but we can count what happens to it.

Privacy forbids reading the payload, not instrumenting state transitions and parse outcomes for operators. The defect cases (e, f) must be visible to us and invisible to the visitor at the same time — observability and disclosure are different budgets.

V.  THE HONEST AXIS IS TRANSPORT vs TERMINAL

Distinguish “couldn’t reach us” from “nothing to show” — never the six flavors of nothing.

A network blip must not read as “gone forever” (the #3424 client fix). The resolution: keep the transport-vs-terminal split on the client; make the terminal class itself uniform on the server. Two different efforts, one coherent rule.

VI.  ORACLES LIVE IN ARCHITECTURE, NOT COPY

A leak in the datastore beats a leak in the response.

The strongest leak found (F1) is a state mutation on a passive visit — it survives CDNs, timing equalization, and uniform error bodies because it lives in Redis, not the body. Tone is presentation; oracles are structure. Audit the side effects, not just the words.

The Five Invariants

For the six realities to be indistinguishable, the anonymous path must hold all five. These are the acceptance criteria for any change to this surface.

1

Indistinguishability

Byte-identical response across all six non-viewable realities — same status (one 404), body, size, and headers. No per-reality sub-code, 422, or 403.

2

Constant-time

Latency independent of existence and reality. Equalize crypto, DB writes, and the passphrase KDF; never short-circuit early on “never existed.”

3

No metadata in transport

no-store; no ETag/Age/Last-Modified; no ID echo; no brand resolution before viewability; no conditional redirect on existence.

4

Side-effect-free probing

A passive visit must not transition new→previewed or consume the view. State advances only on the explicit reveal action. Enforced since #3633 (2026-07-04) — GET is a safe method; accesses land as receipt-timeline telemetry instead.

5

Uniform accounting

Rate-limit, throttle, and lockout debit hits and misses identically — so the limiter itself reveals nothing about validity.

6

Recipient disclosure ⇒ non-server-verifiable

Added round 2. A legitimate-recipient affordance may reveal more — but only client-side, and only if it can never round-trip to the server. The moment it is server-verifiable it becomes a covert read-receipt the sender never consented to.

Reality Check — Gap Status

The design above is the target. A red-team pass found four of its load-bearing invariants contradicted by shipping code. Status ledger (re-verified against source 2026-07-04): F1 is closed by #3633 (PR #3635); F2–F5 and F9–F10 remain open; F11 is half-closed (the passive-fetch half) — see each card.

F1

A passive page load mutated state (new → previewed) — CLOSED

Closed by #3633 (PR #3635, merged 2026-07-04). A metadata GET no longer advances the secret’s lifecycle; state moves only on a genuine reveal or burn, and the old unconditional save_fields(:state) became an atomic, non-creating CAS on the reveal path. Passive accesses are recorded as telemetry on the receipt’s timeline — owner-visible, never in the anonymous response. Invariant 4 now holds. show_secret.rb (AccessTelemetry), secret_state_management.rb#previewed!

CLOSED
F2

Four status codes across the realities, not one

404 (missing/consumed/expired) / 422 (schema reject) / 403 (guest routes disabled) / 429 (rate-limit lockout). Status alone distinguishes several realities. Violates invariant 1. otto_hooks.rb:54–84

HIGH
F3

Passphrase rate-limit branch is a timing + existence oracle

A non-existent ID short-circuits before any work; a real, passphrase-protected secret runs a Redis pipeline — a measurable delta. A 429 lockout is something only a real protected ID can ever return. Violates invariants 1 & 2.

HIGH
F4

The terminal screen isn’t actually static

The SPA always fires the GET on mount and keys its view off the raw HTTP status — so even with static HTML, the second XHR is the oracle (and per F2 its status varies by reality). Violates invariant 1.

HIGH
F5

Branding resolves from the host before viewability

Branded vs canonical chrome is chosen from the bootstrap/host, so the branded screen renders for any ID — including typos — confirming “tenant X exists on this host.” Violates invariant 3 (no brand before 200).

MED
F1 closed (2026-07-04). The “fix first” item shipped: #3633 made GET a safe method — link-preview crawlers (Slack unfurl, Outlook Safe Links, AV scanners) can no longer flip new→previewed before the human clicks, and probing no longer rewrites the sender’s ground truth. Fix next: F2/F4 — one uniform status across the terminal realities, because the SPA’s always-fired XHR keys its view off that status; F2 and F4 close or stay open together.

Round 2 — Gaps Beyond the Server Response

The first round modeled one server response to a hostile prober. These gaps live off that surface — in the URL, the client, the browser, and a second endpoint — and are invisible to the byte-identical-response invariant.

F6

The URL is the bearer secret — and it leaks

The server is zero-knowledge; the URL is not. The id in the path/fragment escapes via browser history, bfcache, and Referer to fonts/analytics/Sentry/CDN logs. Fix: Referrer-Policy: no-referrer on reveal routes; prefer fragment over path; ensure Sentry/analytics never capture window.location; strip the id from edge logs.

HIGH
F7

bfcache / back-button replays plaintext after destruction

Reveal is an in-place SPA mutation; back/forward or bfcache re-renders already-revealed plaintext from JS memory with no server round-trip — after the secret is destroyed. Fix: Cache-Control: no-store on the reveal response; a pagehide/bfcache listener that nulls record.value; clear-on-unmount.

HIGH
F8

Client plaintext lingers (DOM / clipboard / password manager)

“Destroyed on the server” ≠ destroyed on the client. Plaintext reaches the live DOM, the OS clipboard (incl. cross-device clipboard history), password managers, and screenshots, with no client scrubbing. Fix: scrub record.value on unmount/pagehide; “clear from screen” + clipboard guidance.

MED–HIGH
F9

POST /api/v3/guest/receipts is a batch status oracle

It returns is_previewed/is_revealed/is_burned to whoever holds the receipt id — a real sender-detectability channel that bypasses the single-response uniformity entirely. Fix: audit that receipt ids are unguessable and underivable from the link; uniform/constant-time on unknown ids; treat it as an in-scope surface, not an exception.

HIGH
F10

Third-party-request fingerprint

If Sentry/analytics/branded-asset fetches fire on the 200 branch but not the 404 (or vice-versa), a network observer distinguishes realities by which requests fire — defeating byte-identical responses. Generalizes F5. Fix: identical request fan-out across viewable and not-viewable branches; resolve branding only after viewability.

MED
F11

Link-preview bots consume the secret in-transit

Email scanners / unfurlers (Slack, Outlook Safe Links, AV) auto-fetch the URL and can spend the one view before the human arrives — F1 escalated across the email boundary. Half-closed by #3633: the passive GET is now strictly side-effect-free, so a bare fetch spends nothing. Remaining: the destructive reveal is still reachable via GET…continue=true rather than a human-gated POST (A4); a bot that executes the SPA and fires that request still consumes the view.

PARTIAL

The Combination Oracle

The most important round-2 finding, and a standing rule for this surface.

Oracle-safe in isolation ≠ oracle-safe in combination. Features judged safe one at a time can compose into a leak. Worked example: A2 (a local “I revealed it” flag) + F9 (the /guest/receipts endpoint, which returns is_revealed to a receipt-id holder) + A1’s request-presence delta together form a third-party-interception detector — letting someone distinguish “I consumed it” from “someone else consumed it,” the exact intended-vs-actual-recipient question. Rule: evaluate any recipient-side feature as a set with F9 and the network-request pattern, never standalone.

Client-Side Opportunities (A-series)

Capabilities only the legitimate recipient’s browser can power, with zero new server disclosure — the pole the threat-only frame couldn’t see. Verdicts reflect the adversarial review: one idea is actively dangerous and dropped.

IdeaAsymmetry it usesWhy safe / the constraintVerdict
A1 · Recipient proof-of-receipt The reveal instant + winning the race On re-visit, “You opened this on DATE on this device.” Must still call the server every visit and render the hint from the uniform response — skipping the call on a local hit is itself a network oracle. sessionStorage; opaque token, not the secret id; no content. WITH CONSTRAINTS
A2 · “Consumed on this device” flag This browser got the 200 + plaintext Re-visit warns “you already opened this” instead of a cold terminal screen. Local flag, never transmitted. See the combination-oracle rule. WITH CONSTRAINTS
A3 · Client-side plaintext digest Only the recipient ever saw the plaintext Dangerous: a one-way digest of a low-entropy secret (PIN, OTP, seedphrase, sk-/ghp_ token, short password) is brute-forceable offline — it resurrects the destroyed secret past the sender’s TTL and defeats zero-knowledge. Net-negative below ~60 bits (the common case). No constraint makes it safe. DROPPED
A4 · Human-intent gate before reveal Only a human should fire the destructive POST Blocks accidental prefetch/scanner consumption (F11) — not a bot defense (headless clicks trivially). Must stay fully keyboard/AT-accessible; no third-party challenge (that would create F10). WITH CONSTRAINTS
A5 · Opt-in localStorage vs default ephemeral Recipient owns their device-trust choice Default sessionStorage protects shared devices; persistence is explicit opt-in. Opt-in copy must never contain content or a digest. SHIP-SAFE

The recipient currently holds nothing persistent; the existing client receipt store (localReceiptStore.ts, sessionStorage) serves the sender. These reuse that infrastructure for the other party.

Recipient-Artifact Safety Rails

Every A-series feature is leak-free only if all of these hold at once. Break one and it re-arms an oracle the first round closed.

1 · Never server-verifiable. No artifact may round-trip to /guest/receipts or any endpoint — else it becomes a covert read-receipt to the sender.
2 · Always still call the server. Render the local hint from the uniform response; never skip the request on a local hit (request-presence is a network oracle).
3 · Separate namespace, opaque key. Apart from the sender’s onetimeReceiptCache; store an opaque local token, not the link-derivable secret id.
4 · Status only — never content or digest. No plaintext and no hash of plaintext. This is what kills A3.
5 · Default ephemeral. sessionStorage by default; localStorage only on explicit opt-in (protects shared devices).
6 · TTL + count-capped + one-tap erase, disclosed. Durable client persistence is PII — GDPR notice and right-to-erasure apply.

Still Open — Negative Space After Two Rounds

Named so the next pass starts here rather than re-deriving it. Two rounds have not examined:

  • The sender side & the original anxiety. Round 2 went recipient-heavy; the sender’s “did the right person read it?” is still under-served, and the receipt page reportedly shows a generated value for ~60s (a sender-side replay window) that was not audited.
  • The passphrase as its own channel. has_passphrase is client-stored and in receipt status; the passphrase often travels in the same email as the link, yet is not modeled as a separate secret with its own leakage.
  • Sender == recipient (self-send). Both client stores collide on one origin/device; the “render on re-visit” path vs. the sender’s own pre-existing record is undefined and untested.
  • Locale / i18n & accessibility differentials as oracles. A translated, RTL, or screen-reader-announced terminal string — or a render-timing difference — can distinguish realities even when the HTTP status is uniform.
  • eDiscovery / legal hold. Persisted client artifacts (A1/A2/A5) are discoverable evidence that a specific secret existed and was opened — a threat model neither round treated.
  • A standing combination-oracle pass. Re-run the combination check (above) plus a dedicated sender-side review before shipping any A-series feature.

Recommended Recipient UX

Humane and leak-free. Warmth lives in a static, identical-to-everyone template; org branding, recipient reassurance, and the reveal affordance live only behind a 200.
